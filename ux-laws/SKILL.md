This skill provides a concise reference to the Laws of UX, including each law's name, canonical URL, and a brief description. Use it to ground design and product decisions in well-known behavioral principles.

## Laws of UX Reference

- **Aesthetic-Usability Effect**
  - **URL**: https://lawsofux.com/aesthetic-usability-effect/
  - **Description**: Attractive interfaces are perceived as more usable and pleasant, even when their actual utility is the same.

- **Doherty Threshold**
  - **URL**: https://lawsofux.com/doherty-threshold/
  - **Description**: Systems feel responsive and keep users engaged when they react in under roughly 400 ms.

- **Fitts’s Law**
  - **URL**: https://lawsofux.com/fittss-law/
  - **Description**: The time to select a target depends on its size and distance—bigger and closer targets are faster and easier to hit.

- **Hick’s Law**
  - **URL**: https://lawsofux.com/hicks-law/
  - **Description**: Decision time increases with the number and complexity of choices; reduce or group options to speed decisions.

- **Jakob’s Law**
  - **URL**: https://lawsofux.com/jakobs-law/
  - **Description**: Users prefer products that work like the ones they already know, so reuse familiar interaction patterns.

- **Law of Common Region**
  - **URL**: https://lawsofux.com/law-of-common-region/
  - **Description**: Elements enclosed within the same visual region or container are perceived as related.

- **Law of Prägnanz**
  - **URL**: https://lawsofux.com/law-of-pragnanz/
  - **Description**: People interpret complex images in the simplest way possible, so designs should favor clear, simple structures.

- **Law of Proximity**
  - **URL**: https://lawsofux.com/law-of-proximity/
  - **Description**: Items that are physically close to each other are perceived as belonging to the same group.

- **Law of Similarity**
  - **URL**: https://lawsofux.com/law-of-similarity/
  - **Description**: Elements that share visual characteristics (such as shape, color, or size) are seen as related.

- **Law of Uniform Connectedness**
  - **URL**: https://lawsofux.com/law-of-uniform-connectedness/
  - **Description**: Visually connected elements (through lines, backgrounds, or connectors) are perceived as a stronger group than by proximity alone.

- **Miller’s Law**
  - **URL**: https://lawsofux.com/millers-law/
  - **Description**: People can comfortably hold about 7±2 items in working memory; chunk information to reduce cognitive load.

- **Occam’s Razor**
  - **URL**: https://lawsofux.com/occams-razor/
  - **Description**: When multiple solutions exist, the simplest one that adequately solves the problem is usually best.

- **Pareto Principle (80/20 Rule)**
  - **URL**: https://lawsofux.com/pareto-principle/
  - **Description**: Roughly 80% of outcomes often come from 20% of causes; focus design effort on the most impactful features and flows.

- **Parkinson’s Law**
  - **URL**: https://lawsofux.com/parkinsons-law/
  - **Description**: Work expands to fill the time available; tighter constraints and scopes help teams focus and ship.

- **Peak-End Rule**
  - **URL**: https://lawsofux.com/peak-end-rule/
  - **Description**: People judge an experience mostly by its most intense moment and its end, rather than the average of every moment.

- **Postel’s Law**
  - **URL**: https://lawsofux.com/postels-law/
  - **Description**: Be liberal in what you accept from users (inputs, formats) and conservative in what you output (clear, consistent responses).

- **Serial Position Effect**
  - **URL**: https://lawsofux.com/serial-position-effect/
  - **Description**: Users remember the first and last items in a sequence best; place key actions or information at the beginning or end.

- **Tesler’s Law (Law of Conservation of Complexity)**
  - **URL**: https://lawsofux.com/teslers-law/
  - **Description**: Every system has inherent complexity; you cannot remove it, only decide whether the system or the user carries it.

- **Von Restorff Effect (Isolation Effect)**
  - **URL**: https://lawsofux.com/von-restorff-effect/
  - **Description**: An item that stands out from its surroundings is more likely to be noticed and remembered.

- **Zeigarnik Effect**
  - **URL**: https://lawsofux.com/zeigarnik-effect/
  - **Description**: People remember unfinished or interrupted tasks better than completed ones, which can be used to motivate task completion.

- **Goal-Gradient Effect**
  - **URL**: https://lawsofux.com/goal-gradient-effect/
  - **Description**: Users tend to increase their effort as they perceive themselves getting closer to a goal, making progress indicators highly effective.

## Laws of UX Takeaways

- **Aesthetic-Usability Effect**
  - Visually polished interfaces can hide minor usability flaws and still feel “good enough” in testing.
  - People forgive small issues more easily when the product looks and feels attractive.
  - Investing in visual quality can boost perceived usability, but should not replace real usability work.

- **Doherty Threshold**
  - Keep interactive responses under roughly 400 ms so the interface feels instant and keeps attention.
  - Use progress indicators and micro‑animations to make unavoidable waits feel shorter and more trustworthy.
  - Design for perceived performance, not just raw speed, so users rarely feel like they are waiting.

- **Fitts’s Law**
  - Make key targets large enough and close to where the user’s focus or cursor already is.
  - Ensure comfortable spacing between touch targets to reduce mis-taps, especially on mobile.
  - Place frequently used actions at the edges or corners where they are easier and faster to hit.

- **Hick’s Law**
  - Reduce the number of visible choices at once, especially in moments that require fast decisions.
  - Break complex flows into smaller, guided steps instead of a single overloaded screen.
  - Highlight recommended or default options so users can move forward without analysis paralysis.

- **Jakob’s Law**
  - Reuse established patterns from other familiar products instead of inventing new ones.
  - When redesigning, give users a gentle transition (or temporary fallback) so mental models are not broken overnight.
  - Assume users bring expectations from other tools and design so your interface works “as expected” on first contact.

- **Law of Common Region**
  - Use containers, cards, and panels to show which elements belong together.
  - Backgrounds, borders, and grouping lines can clarify structure even when spacing is tight.
  - Prefer strong visual grouping over relying only on labels or layout hints to explain relationships.

- **Law of Prägnanz**
  - Favor simple, clear compositions over clever but visually complex layouts.
  - Remove decorative detail that doesn’t help comprehension so the main structure is obvious at a glance.
  - Use consistent, repeatable patterns so users can recognize structure without extra cognitive effort.

- **Law of Proximity**
  - Place related items close together and put unrelated items farther apart.
  - Use spacing as a primary way to communicate section boundaries and relationships.
  - Be careful that tight spacing doesn’t accidentally imply a relationship that doesn’t exist.

- **Law of Similarity**
  - Make elements that behave the same look the same (and elements that behave differently look different).
  - Ensure links and navigation controls are visually distinct from plain text.
  - Use shared color, size, or shape to visually tie together components with a common role.

- **Law of Uniform Connectedness**
  - Connect related elements with shared backgrounds, borders, or lines to make their relationship obvious.
  - Use visual connectors (e.g. lines, arrows) to show flow or dependency between items.
  - Group controls of the same function within a single connected region instead of scattering them.

- **Miller’s Law**
  - Chunk complex information into small, meaningful groups instead of long, flat lists.
  - Don’t overfit to the “7±2” number; design for clarity and context rather than a magic item count.
  - Use progressive disclosure so users only hold the information they need for the current step.

- **Occam’s Razor**
  - Prefer the simplest design that fully solves the problem—no simpler, no more complex.
  - Remove elements and options that do not materially help users achieve their goals.
  - Prevent complexity by making straightforward choices early, instead of layering on exceptions later.

- **Pareto Principle (80/20 Rule)**
  - Identify the small set of flows or features that drive most of the value and optimize those first.
  - Don’t over-invest in low-impact edge cases while core journeys still feel rough.
  - Use analytics and research to locate the “vital few” interactions worth disproportionate attention.

- **Parkinson’s Law**
  - Set clear, tight scopes and constraints so tasks and projects do not expand endlessly.
  - Design flows so key actions can be completed quickly, matching users’ time expectations.
  - Use smart defaults and automation (e.g. autofill) to prevent forms and tasks from feeling bloated.

- **Peak-End Rule**
  - Intentionally design standout “peak” moments (e.g. first success, celebration states) in key journeys.
  - Ensure the ending of a flow—confirmation, summary, or follow‑up—feels clear, reassuring, and positive.
  - Fix painful peaks (frustrating errors, confusing steps) because they disproportionately shape memory.

- **Postel’s Law**
  - Accept a wide range of reasonable user input and normalize it behind the scenes.
  - Provide clear, specific feedback for invalid input instead of simply rejecting it.
  - Design tolerant interfaces that are robust to imperfect data, devices, and network conditions.

- **Serial Position Effect**
  - Place the most important actions or information at the beginning or end of lists and sequences.
  - Reserve middle positions for less critical options that don’t need to be remembered.
  - Use ordering deliberately; don’t rely on arbitrary or alphabetical sequences for key menus.

- **Tesler’s Law (Law of Conservation of Complexity)**
  - Decide which complexity the system will absorb so the user experiences a simpler surface.
  - Invest engineering and design effort to hide inevitable complexity behind smart defaults and automation.
  - Don’t assume users are rational experts; design for messy, real-world behavior and constraints.

- **Von Restorff Effect (Isolation Effect)**
  - Make primary actions and critical information stand out visually from surrounding content.
  - Use emphasis sparingly so that only truly important elements compete for attention.
  - Combine multiple cues (size, color, weight, iconography) for emphasis, not color alone.

- **Zeigarnik Effect**
  - Show clear progress indicators so users feel momentum and are motivated to finish tasks.
  - Consider giving users a small “head start” on progress bars or checklists to encourage completion.
  - Hint at additional content or steps in a way that invites exploration without feeling manipulative.

- **Goal-Gradient Effect**
  - Make progress tangible (steps, percentage, checklists) so users see themselves getting closer to the goal.
  - Highlight the final steps of a flow to encourage users to finish instead of dropping off.
  - Use milestones and rewards to accelerate effort as users approach completion.
